Transition and deployment are critical phases in the system engineering lifecycle, involving the installation and integration of a verified system into operational use. Different organizations and standards offer diverse perspectives on what constitutes transition, but they generally converge on certain key activities. Below are the main activities involved in the transition and deployment process, along with further elaboration on each item:

  1. Develop a Deployment/Transition Strategy: Planning for transition activities should ideally commence early in the system engineering life cycle, although it can be carried out concurrently with realization activities. This phase involves formulating a comprehensive strategy that outlines how the system will be smoothly and effectively transitioned into operational use. It includes considering lower-level activities such as installation, checkout, integration, and testing, which are crucial to demonstrate the system's functionality, operational viability, and adherence to contractual specifications. To ensure efficient implementation, clear criteria, responsibilities, and procedures for these activities must be established and agreed upon during the planning phase.

  2. Develop Plans for Transitioning Systems: Transition plans are essential to move the system or its incremental capabilities into operational use and support. These plans must align with the overall transition strategy and receive approval from relevant stakeholders. A crucial aspect of planning involves formulating a support strategy, which may encompass organic support infrastructures, contractor logistics support, or other support sources. Defining the levels of support required is also vital, as it influences other transition planning activities and product design considerations. Coordination with other activities like installation, integration, and verification and validation (V&V) is a critical component of the transition plan.

  3. Installation: Installation refers to the physical implementation of the system, including connecting interfaces to other systems (electrical, computer, security, and software). Proper planning documents the complexity of the system, environmental conditions in the operational environment, interface specifications, and safety-related human factors requirements. In cases where real-world conditions necessitate changes in installation requirements, they should be documented and communicated to relevant stakeholders.

  4. Integration: System integration activities generally take place before installation, but additional steps may be required during the transition phase, especially if the system is being delivered incrementally. Integration ensures that individual components work harmoniously together as a cohesive system and function as intended in the operational setting.

  5. Verification and Validation (V&V): This stage involves performing physical, electrical, and mechanical checks to verify that the system has been appropriately installed. Acceptance tests may be conducted after delivery as part of the V&V process. Various types of acceptance tests, such as On-site Acceptance Tests (OSAT), Field Acceptance Tests, and Operational Test and Evaluation (OT&E), may be used to demonstrate the system's performance and effectiveness.

  6. Evaluate the readiness of the system to transition into operations: Transition criteria specified in the transition plan are used to objectively assess the system's readiness for operational deployment. The activities associated with integration, verification, and validation provide valuable insights to gauge whether the system meets the defined transition criteria.

  7. Analyze the results of transition activities and take necessary actions: Analysis of transition activities may identify areas requiring additional actions or improvements. This stage allows for the refinement of future transition activities and ensures continuous improvement in the transition and deployment process.

In certain scenarios, additional considerations and activities come into play:

  • System Run-In: After successful completion of acceptance tests, the system may undergo a run-in period during which its reliability, maintainability, and integrated logistics support (ILS) deliverables are verified under actual field conditions.

  • Phasing-In/Phasing-Out: When a new system replaces an existing one, proper planning for phasing out the old system is essential to minimize disruption and ensure operational readiness. For multi-user or complex systems, phase-by-phase introduction can be considered, along with establishing a feedback system from users for operational improvements.

Transition and deployment are complex endeavors that require careful planning, coordination, and evaluation to ensure the successful integration of systems into their operational environments. By following well-defined strategies and plans, organizations can mitigate risks and achieve seamless system transitions, ultimately leading to operational success.
