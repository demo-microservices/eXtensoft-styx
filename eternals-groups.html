Project

  1. Funding

    • Definition: Funding refers to the allocation of financial resources required to execute the project from start to finish.
    • Significance: Adequate funding is critical in every phase of the SDLC, from initial discovery and planning to deployment and maintenance. Insufficient funding can cause project delays, cut corners, or even lead to project termination. It's especially vital during the "Planning & Risk Mitigation" and "Construction & Migration" phases where resource allocation is decided.

  2. Estimates

    • Definition: Estimates encompass the anticipated time, financial, and human resources needed to complete the project.
    • Significance: In the "Purpose & Discovery" and "Planning & Risk Mitigation" phases, estimates help in setting realistic goals and timelines. Wrong estimates can derail the project in later phases, leading to scope creep, delayed deliveries, and cost overruns.

  3. Signoffs

    • Definition: Signoffs are formal approvals provided by key stakeholders, confirming that a particular phase or deliverable meets the specified criteria and can proceed.
    • Significance: Signoffs are essential at every major milestone or phase transition in the SDLC. They ensure that there's a consensus that the project is meeting its objectives and can proceed to the next phase. Most crucial during the "Planning & Risk Mitigation" and "Deployment & Stabilization" stages.

  4. Stakeholders

    • Definition: Stakeholders are individuals or groups with a vested interest in the success of the project. They can range from team members and managers to clients and end-users.
    • Significance: Stakeholder involvement is crucial throughout the SDLC but becomes particularly important during the "Purpose & Discovery" and "Retrospective" phases. Their input helps to fine-tune requirements, priorities, and ensures that the final product meets expectations.

  5. Risk Management

    • Definition: Risk Management involves identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks that might jeopardize the project.
    • Significance: Effective risk management is essential during all phases but is pivotal during "Planning & Risk Mitigation." Anticipating and planning for potential risks can prevent costly setbacks later in the project lifecycle, such as during "Construction & Migration" or "Deployment & Stabilization."

Each of these project perspectives serves as a lens through which the SDLC can be viewed and managed. Their relevance varies across the phases but ignoring any of them could lead to project delays, cost overruns, or outright failure.

Business

  1. Domain E/R Analysis, Expected Business Growth

    • Definition: Domain Entity-Relationship (E/R) Analysis involves understanding the complex relationships among various entities within the business domain. Expected Business Growth refers to the anticipated scale of expansion, in terms of users, data, or transaction volume.
    • Significance: These are primarily important in the "Purpose & Discovery" phase where understanding the business domain and its future growth is critical for shaping the architecture and functionality of the system. Anticipating growth is also important for ensuring scalability in the "Construction & Migration" phase.

  2. User Stories / Use Cases, Personas, Workflows

    • Definition: User Stories and Use Cases describe functionalities from an end-user perspective. Personas are fictional characters representing key user segments. Workflows map the step-by-step process of tasks within the system.
    • Significance: These elements are crucial in the "Purpose & Discovery" and "Planning & Risk Mitigation" phases for capturing detailed requirements. Understanding the users and their interactions with the system ensures that the final product will meet its intended purpose and be user-friendly. Workflows help in defining process optimizations during the "Construction & Migration" phase.

  3. Prioritized Backlog

    • Definition: A Prioritized Backlog is a list of tasks, features, and requirements that are ranked based on their importance, value, and impact on the business.
    • Significance: The backlog comes into play mainly during the "Planning & Risk Mitigation" phase, where it helps guide the allocation of resources and timelines. It remains relevant through the "Construction & Migration" phase as development progresses and priorities may need to be adjusted. It's also reviewed in the "Retrospective" phase for lessons learned.

Just like with the "Project" category, these "Business" perspectives are vital at different stages of the SDLC. Their importance lies in ensuring that the technical solutions being developed are tightly aligned with business goals, user needs, and future growth prospects. Neglecting these perspectives could lead to a product that is technically sound but fails to meet business objectives or user expectations.

Technical Alignment (business value)

  1. Mandates, Compliance, Governance

    • Definition: Mandates refer to obligatory directives often from a higher authority. Compliance refers to adhering to regulations and standards that could be legal, security-based, or industry-specific. Governance pertains to the framework for managing and overseeing the software project to ensure it aligns with business and technical strategies.
    • Significance: These elements come into play predominantly in the "Purpose & Discovery" and "Planning & Risk Mitigation" phases. They help to set the "rules of the road" for what technologies, methodologies, and practices must be followed. Ignoring these could result in legal issues and could disrupt alignment with business objectives. They are also revisited during the "Retrospective" phase to ensure ongoing alignment with governance models.

  2. Adjacent Systems, Dependencies

    • Definition: Adjacent Systems refer to other software systems or services that interact with or are dependent upon the project. Dependencies are the relationships between this project and other initiatives, libraries, or systems.
    • Significance: These aspects are vital during the "Purpose & Discovery" and "Construction & Migration" phases. Understanding how your system will interact with others is key to defining interfaces and ensuring seamless integration. This is essential for business value as disconnected or poorly integrated systems can create inefficiencies or errors.

  3. Architecture/Design

    • Definition: This refers to the high-level structuring of the system, breaking it down into manageable modules, and deciding how these modules will interact with each other.
    • Significance: Architecture and design are central to the "Planning & Risk Mitigation" phase and continue to be refined during the "Construction & Migration" phase. A well-thought-out architecture is necessary for building a scalable, maintainable, and effective system. It ensures the technical alignment with business needs, thereby providing business value.

This category focuses on ensuring that technical efforts are not just technically sound but are also aligned with business objectives and constraints. The failure to consider these aspects can result in a system that, while technically excellent, could be misaligned with business goals, not compliant with mandatory regulations, or poorly integrated with other critical systems.

Construction

  1. Team Composition, Personnel, Environments

    • Definition: This refers to the allocation and arrangement of human resources and the different development, testing, and production environments that will be used.
    • Significance: These considerations are pivotal mainly in the "Planning & Risk Mitigation" phase but have ongoing relevance. The team's skills and numbers must be appropriate for the project scope, and the environments must be set up to support development, testing, and deployment effectively. Inadequate team composition or poorly set up environments can introduce significant risks and delays.

  2. Sprints, Milestones, Deliverables

    • Definition: These are specific chunks of work, interim objectives, and the actual products or features that need to be delivered by specific deadlines.
    • Significance: These concepts are central to the "Construction & Migration" and "Deployment & Stabilization" phases. By breaking the project down into sprints and milestones, the team can manage work more effectively and measure progress more accurately. Poorly defined sprints, milestones, or deliverables can lead to scope creep, delays, and decreased quality.

  3. Delivery & Configuration Management

    • Definition: This involves the systematic approach to building, packaging, and distributing software for deployment, along with tracking configurations.
    • Significance: These elements are vital during the "Deployment & Stabilization" phase. Ensuring that software is built and deployed in a controlled manner and that configurations are managed are critical for the stability and reliability of the system.

  4. Quality & User Acceptance

    • Definition: This refers to ensuring that the software meets defined quality standards and criteria, and that it's accepted by the end-users or customers as meeting their needs and requirements.
    • Significance: These are essential for the "Deployment & Stabilization" and "Retrospective" phases. It's the final validation that what has been built meets the required quality standards and fulfills the user needs. Failing to meet quality standards or user expectations can result in the failure of the project.

In the "Construction" category, it's about turning the plans and designs into a working software system while managing the complexities and challenges that inevitably arise in software development projects. Each of these elements serves to mitigate those challenges and guide the project to a successful conclusion.
