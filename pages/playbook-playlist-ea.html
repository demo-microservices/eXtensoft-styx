Introduction to EA Playbook Plays

  • Application Value Assessment:

    • Evaluate the business value of each application. Consider factors like how critical it is to operations, how often it's used, and the impact on the business if it were to be retired.

  • Technical Debt Quantification:

    • Determine the technical debt associated with maintaining the application on the nearly obsolete technology stack. This includes considering security vulnerabilities, integration challenges, and scalability issues.

  • Migration Feasibility:

    • Investigate the feasibility, costs, and benefits of migrating the application to a modern technology stack versus retiring it and potentially replacing it with a new solution.

  • Operational Cost Analysis:

    • Examine the ongoing operational costs of the application, including licensing, support, and infrastructure costs, and compare this to the cost of retirement and potential replacement.

  • Stakeholder Impact and Communication Plan:

    • Understand the impact of application retirement on various stakeholders, both internal and external. Develop a plan to communicate the potential changes, gather feedback, and ensure a smooth transition if the decision to retire is made.

These Plays can guide an organization in making a well-informed and comprehensive decision about the retirement of applications using nearly obsolete technology stacks.

Play: Application Value Assessment

Purpose: To evaluate and determine the business value, relevance, and importance of a given application within the organization's ecosystem.

Target Audience:

  • Enterprise Architects
  • Business Analysts
  • Product Owners
  • Project Managers
  • Department Heads and Business Unit Leaders

Checklist:

  • Current Business Relevance:

    • Determine the current utility of the application in serving business needs. Is it still actively used, and does it fulfill a unique function?

  • User Activity Analysis:

    • Assess the frequency and nature of usage. Which departments or roles rely heavily on the application? Are there any patterns indicating declining or increasing usage?

  • Financial Analysis:

    • Review the direct and indirect costs associated with the application, such as licensing, maintenance, and support. Compare this to the perceived value or ROI the application delivers.

  • Operational Efficiency:

    • Evaluate the application's performance metrics, downtime history, and maintenance challenges. How often are there disruptions, and what's the impact?

  • Data Criticality Assessment:

    • Determine the importance and relevance of the data within the application. Does it house critical business data, and if so, how often is this data accessed?

  • Integration Importance:

    • Identify other systems or applications that are integrated with or dependent on this application. What would be the impact on these systems if this application were retired or replaced?

  • Feedback from Users:

    • Collect feedback from regular users regarding the application's utility, performance, and any challenges they face.

  • Comparison with Modern Alternatives:

    • Investigate if there are newer applications or tools available that could serve the same purpose but offer better functionality, efficiency, or cost-effectiveness.

Key Questions:

  • What strategic business objectives does the application currently support?
  • Are there newer technologies or platforms that could deliver the same value more efficiently?
  • How would the business be affected if the application were to be unavailable for an extended period?
  • What are the most common challenges or complaints users have about the application?
  • What is the total cost of ownership (TCO) of the application, and is this justifiable based on its current value to the organization?

Desired Outcomes:

  • A comprehensive understanding of the application's current value and relevance to the organization.
  • Insights into potential challenges or inefficiencies related to the application.
  • A foundation for decision-making regarding potential upgrades, replacements, or retirement of the application.

Play: Technical Debt Quantification

Purpose:
To identify, measure, and prioritize the technical debt present within an application or system, allowing for informed decisions regarding mitigation, refactoring, or potential retirement.

Target Audience:

  • Technical Leads
  • Software Engineers and Developers
  • Quality Assurance Engineers
  • Enterprise Architects
  • Project Managers

Checklist:

  • Codebase Analysis:

    • Utilize static code analysis tools to identify code smells, code complexity, duplicate code, and potential security vulnerabilities.

  • Historical Bug Tracking:

    • Examine the defect tracking system to understand the frequency, severity, and types of bugs that have been recurrent. This often hints at underlying technical debt.

  • Performance Bottlenecks:

    • Identify areas where inefficient code or outdated libraries/tools result in sluggish performance or resource overuse.

  • Dependency Review:

    • Analyze third-party libraries, frameworks, and tools for obsolescence or known issues. Identify if updates are available or if there's a need to migrate to a more modern alternative.

  • Documentation Adequacy:

    • Assess the comprehensiveness and accuracy of current documentation. Incomplete or outdated documentation can represent a form of technical debt.

  • Architectural Review:

    • Check for architectural inconsistencies, rigidity, or issues that prevent scaling or evolving the system easily.

  • Feedback from Development Team:

    • Interview developers and testers to gain insights into challenges faced during feature development, bug fixes, or testing due to existing technical debt.

  • Cost Estimation:

    • Based on the identified debt, estimate the effort and cost required for remediation.

Key Questions:

  • What parts of the codebase are most prone to errors and require frequent fixes?
  • Are there any external dependencies that are no longer supported or have better alternatives available?
  • How much time does the team spend addressing issues related to technical debt versus new feature development?
  • What are the potential risks if the identified technical debt is not addressed?
  • How does the existing technical debt impact the end-users in terms of application performance, usability, or reliability?

Desired Outcomes:

  • A prioritized list of technical debt items, categorized by severity and impact.
  • An understanding of the associated risks and costs for each item of technical debt.
  • A foundation for planning and budgeting for technical debt remediation activities, allowing for a strategic approach to improve software health and maintainability.

Play: Migration Feasibility

Purpose:
To systematically evaluate the practicality, challenges, risks, and benefits associated with migrating a legacy application to a newer technology stack or platform.

Target Audience:

  • Enterprise Architects
  • Technical Leads
  • Project Managers
  • Business Analysts
  • DevOps Engineers

Checklist:

  1. Current System Analysis:

    • Document the application's existing architecture, technologies used, integrations, data flows, and any proprietary or custom solutions in use.

  2. Migration Goal Definition:

    • Clearly define the objectives of the migration, such as performance improvements, scalability, enhanced features, or support for future growth.

  3. Technology Stack Evaluation:

    • Compare the existing technology stack with potential replacements, considering factors like performance, scalability, support, and compatibility.

  4. Data Migration Challenges:

    • Assess the complexity of migrating data from the legacy system to the new platform, considering data integrity, volume, format differences, and any necessary transformations.

  5. Integration and Interoperability:

    • Review current integrations with other systems or services, determining how these will be managed or replaced in the new environment.

  6. Risk Assessment:

    • Identify potential pitfalls, challenges, or showstoppers in the migration process. Consider both technical and business risks.

  7. Cost and Time Estimation:

    • Provide a ballpark figure for the migration's time and cost, including unexpected contingencies and post-migration support.

  8. Stakeholder Feedback:

    • Engage with users, developers, and other key stakeholders to gather input, concerns, or insights that might affect the migration's feasibility.

Key Questions:

  1. What are the main drivers pushing for the migration, and how do they align with organizational goals?
  2. Are there any components of the legacy system that cannot be migrated or replaced?
  3. How will the migration affect end-users in terms of downtime, changed functionalities, or learning curves?
  4. Are there skills or knowledge gaps within the team that need to be addressed for a successful migration?
  5. How will success be measured post-migration?

Desired Outcomes:

  • A comprehensive report detailing the feasibility of migrating the legacy system, including potential benefits, risks, costs, and timeframes.
  • Clear insights into potential roadblocks or challenges, enabling proactive planning.
  • An informed foundation for decision-making, allowing stakeholders to determine whether to proceed with the migration, seek alternatives, or maintain the status quo.

Operational Cost

Play: Operational Cost Analysis

Purpose:
To assess the financial implications of operating an application, providing a clear picture of its ongoing costs, and evaluating opportunities for cost optimization.

Target Audience:

  • Enterprise Architects
  • Financial Analysts
  • IT Managers
  • Operations Managers
  • Technical Leads

Checklist:

  1. Infrastructure Costs:

    • Calculate the costs related to servers, storage, networking, and other hardware requirements.
    • Include cloud services fees if the application is hosted on a cloud platform.

  2. Licensing and Software Fees:

    • Evaluate the costs of third-party software, licenses, or tools used in the application's operation.

  3. Maintenance and Support:

    • Quantify the expenses of regular maintenance tasks, patching, updates, and support tickets.

  4. Operational Personnel:

    • Calculate the cost of manpower needed to keep the application running, including salaries, benefits, and training.

  5. Downtime and Unavailability Costs:

    • Estimate potential financial losses during unplanned outages, considering both direct costs and reputational damages.

  6. Integration and Interoperability Costs:

    • Determine costs associated with integrating the application with other systems, including middleware, API usage fees, and data exchange mechanisms.

  7. Security and Compliance:

    • Evaluate expenses related to securing the application, including firewalls, intrusion detection systems, audits, and compliance certifications.

  8. Backup, Recovery, and Redundancy:

    • Analyze costs related to backup solutions, disaster recovery preparations, and any redundancy measures.

Key Questions:

  1. What are the largest cost drivers for the application's operation, and are there areas ripe for optimization?
  2. How do operational costs compare to the application's delivered value?
  3. Are there any foreseeable events or changes that could significantly impact operational costs in the future?
  4. Are there any existing service-level agreements (SLAs) or contracts that dictate certain operational expenditures?
  5. What is the cost trend over time, and can any spikes or anomalies be explained?

Desired Outcomes:

  • A detailed breakdown of the operational costs associated with the application.
  • Insights into potential areas for cost-saving or reallocation.
  • A basis for strategic decisions regarding application retirement, migration, or re-architecture based on financial feasibility.
