Lessons Learned

  1. If it ain't pretty, it don't work
  2. Make it work, make it work better, make it generic
  3. Wheel within a wheel
  4. Don't fence me in
  5. A man's gotta think about the future
  6. Doctor, Doctor!
  7. Where could I be going wrong?
  8. What is the thing that it is?
  9. Don't jump to solutions
  10. Easy is expensive
  11. A stitch in design saves nine
  12. Monkey see, monkey do
  13. Don't be stupid, be curious
  14. Be the Invisible Man: Clarity, not Control
  15. Don't go near there!

This list offers guidance, wisdom, warnings and encouragement in various aspects of the craft. They touch on design, development, collaboration, ethics, and continuous learning, providing a comprehensive and thoughtful approach to building software. By embracing and internalizing these principles, developers and organizations can create more effective, sustainable, and innovative software solutions that align with both current needs and future growth.

  1. If it ain't pretty, it don't work: Usability and aesthetics are critical to user adoption and effective usage. An application must not only function well but also be intuitive and visually appealing. If it's unattractive or challenging to use, it may be rejected by users, rendering its technical merits irrelevant.

    Usability and aesthetics are critical to user adoption and effective usage. An application must not only function well but also be intuitive and visually appealing. If it's unattractive or challenging to use, it may be rejected by users, rendering its technical merits irrelevant.

  2. Make it work, make it work better, make it generic: This principle advocates for an iterative approach to development. Start by solving the immediate problem, then refine and optimize, and finally, if applicable, generalize to create reusable components or libraries. This approach fosters incremental improvements and leverages the concept of reusability, fundamental in software engineering.

    By breaking down the development process into stages, developers can focus on solving the immediate problem, refine and optimize the solution, and then generalize it for future use. This incremental approach encourages continuous improvement, allowing developers to learn from each iteration. Building reusable components or libraries promotes efficiency and consistency, allowing teams to leverage previous work, which accelerates development and reduces redundancy.

  3. No loops within loops, no I/O within loops: A warning against practices that can lead to computationally expensive or inefficient code. Nested loops or performing I/O within loops can create performance bottlenecks. This principle promotes writing efficient code that scales well, keeping resource utilization in check.

    Inefficient code practices, such as nested loops or performing input/output (I/O) operations within loops, can result in significant performance issues. Nested loops increase computational complexity, while I/O within loops can cause delays and blockage. Understanding how to optimize code and avoid these pitfalls leads to more scalable and responsive applications. This principle emphasizes the importance of efficiency and resource management, which are crucial for maintaining optimal performance.

  4. Don't fence me in: Encourages developers to think freely and emphasizes outcomes rather than prescriptive methods. This aligns with agile and modern management practices, fostering innovation and adaptability. It's about empowering individuals with trust and autonomy, leading to more creative and effective solutions.

    Rigid and prescriptive methodologies can stifle creativity and innovation. This principle encourages developers and teams to embrace flexibility, autonomy, and trust, which can foster a more open and innovative environment. By focusing on outcomes and empowering individuals to approach problems creatively, organizations can cultivate a culture of continuous improvement and adaptability, leading to better solutions and overall growth.

  5. A man's gotta think about the future: This emphasizes foresight in design and development. Think about scalability, potential future changes, and long-term sustainability without falling into the trap of over-engineering. It's a call for balance and vision, aligning present needs with future growth.

    Planning for the future is essential in software development, but it must be balanced to avoid over-engineering or adding unnecessary complexity. Considering scalability, maintainability, and adaptability from the outset ensures that the system can evolve to meet future requirements without significant rework. This foresight, coupled with an understanding of current needs, ensures that the software is well-positioned to grow and adapt over time.

  6. Always get a second opinion: Collaboration and diversity of thought are key to avoiding tunnel vision in design and development. By seeking various perspectives, you enrich the solution and mitigate potential biases or blind spots. It fosters a more robust and well-considered approach to problem-solving.

    Collaboration and peer review are invaluable in identifying potential issues or weaknesses that might be overlooked by a single individual. By actively seeking input from various stakeholders and team members with diverse expertise, developers can create more robust and well-rounded solutions. This collaborative approach also fosters a sense of teamwork and shared ownership, leading to higher quality software and a more cohesive team.

  7. Where could I be going wrong?: This encourages self-awareness and critical examination of one's decisions and assumptions. Regularly questioning and reflecting helps uncover hidden flaws, biases, or oversights, leading to more robust and well-rounded solutions.

    Continuous reflection and critical thinking enable developers to identify potential flaws or biases in their decisions, assumptions, or approaches. By consistently questioning and reassessing, developers can ensure that they are on the right track, uncovering hidden weaknesses and avoiding potential pitfalls. This introspective approach leads to more thoughtful and effective problem-solving, aligning closely with the principles of continuous improvement and self-awareness.

  8. What is the thing that it is?: Understanding the essence of the problem domain is vital. Deep and insightful analysis helps in crafting solutions that are not only effective today but adaptable to future changes. It's about building a strong foundation that can respond to evolving business needs.

    Understanding the core problem and the domain in which the software operates is crucial for building relevant and adaptable solutions. By delving into the essence of the problem and analyzing its underlying factors, developers can create software that is aligned with the business needs and flexible enough to adapt to future changes. This approach ensures a solid foundation that not only addresses immediate requirements but is poised to grow and evolve with the business landscape.

  9. Don't jump to solutions: Coding without proper understanding or analysis can lead to misguided solutions. This principle advocates for thorough problem analysis, understanding the root cause, and designing with intent before jumping into implementation.

    Taking the time to thoroughly understand a problem before diving into coding ensures that the solution is well-aligned with the actual needs and constraints of the system. By investing in careful analysis, design, and planning, developers are more likely to create effective and maintainable solutions that address the root cause of the problem. This approach helps avoid wasted effort on misguided solutions, emphasizes thoughtful design, and reinforces the value of a well-considered development process.

  10. The easy way is the most expensive: Often, shortcuts or 'quick fixes' may lead to technical debt or other hidden costs. This principle encourages well-thought-out solutions that consider long-term impacts and sustainability, even if they require more initial effort.

    Opting for shortcuts or expedient solutions might provide immediate relief but often leads to technical debt or other hidden costs down the line. These "quick fixes" may complicate maintenance, limit scalability, or create dependencies that hinder future development. By prioritizing long-term sustainability and well-architected solutions, developers can build software that stands the test of time, reducing the overall cost and complexity in the long run.

  11. A stitch in design saves nine: Early attention to design can save significant time and effort in the later stages of development. Thoughtful design reduces the likelihood of rework and aligns with the broader vision, minimizing wasteful iterations.

    Investing time and effort in thoughtful design at the early stages of a project can prevent costly and time-consuming rework later on. By aligning the design with the overall vision and objectives, developers can create a coherent and well-structured solution that meets the project's needs. This proactive approach to design not only saves time and resources but also enhances the quality and maintainability of the software.

  12. Monkey see, monkey do: Emphasizes learning from others and leveraging existing solutions, patterns, or practices. Reinventing the wheel is often unnecessary. Utilizing proven approaches allows you to stand on the shoulders of giants and foster a culture of continuous learning.

    Learning from others and building upon existing knowledge and solutions promotes efficiency and continuous growth. By leveraging proven patterns, practices, or existing solutions, developers can avoid redundancy and benefit from the collective wisdom and experience of the broader community. This principle encourages a culture of collaboration and continuous learning, fostering professional growth and the sharing of best practices.

  13. Don't be stupid: wallow in curiosity: Encourages intellectual curiosity, continuous learning, and embracing complexity. Staying inquisitive and open-minded fosters growth and adaptability, essential in a rapidly changing field like technology.

    Embracing curiosity and a continuous learning mindset ensures that developers stay relevant and adaptable in a rapidly evolving field. By seeking to understand complex problems, exploring new technologies, and engaging with ongoing professional development, developers can grow their skills and contribute more effectively to their projects. This principle emphasizes the importance of intellectual curiosity as a driver of innovation, growth, and adaptability within the software development field.

  14. Be the Invisible Man: Clarity, not Control: Promotes clarity in design, code, and communication over strict control. Clear and understandable code, documentation, and collaboration are more valuable than overly complex or controlling processes. This principle aligns with modern development practices emphasizing simplicity and collaboration.

    Clarity in design, code, and communication fosters a more collaborative and efficient development process. By prioritizing clear and concise code, well-documented design, and open collaboration, teams can work together more effectively and avoid misunderstandings or confusion. This emphasis on clarity over control promotes a more agile and responsive development process, aligning with contemporary practices that value simplicity, transparency, and teamwork.

  15. Don't go near there!: This principle urges developers to be mindful of known problematic areas, pitfalls, or antipatterns. It's about recognizing and avoiding known risks, drawing from both personal experience and collective wisdom. It promotes a thoughtful and risk-aware approach to development.

    Being aware of and avoiding known problematic areas, pitfalls, or antipatterns helps developers navigate the complex landscape of software development more effectively. By learning from past mistakes and drawing upon collective wisdom, developers can anticipate and avoid potential risks or challenges. This risk-aware approach fosters more thoughtful and well-considered development, minimizing the likelihood of encountering known issues or difficulties.

These principles form a robust framework for software development, offering guidance, wisdom, and encouragement in various aspects of the craft. They touch on design, development, collaboration, ethics, and continuous learning, providing a comprehensive and thoughtful approach to building software. By embracing and internalizing these principles, developers and organizations can create more effective, sustainable, and innovative software solutions that align with both current needs and future growth.
