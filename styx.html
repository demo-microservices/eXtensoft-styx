Purpose & Discovery

Objectives

  • Articulate Motivation/Purpose, Goals, Value Proposition: Before you begin, it's essential to clearly define why the project exists, what objectives it aims to achieve, and the value it brings to the organization or users.

  • Identify Stakeholders, Risks, Compliance & Mandate Constraints: Early identification of everyone involved or affected, as well as understanding the legal and organizational boundaries, can mitigate risks and avoid future complications.

  • Estimate Size, Complexity, Cost, Feasibility: Gain a high-level understanding of the scale of the project in terms of size, technical complexity, estimated costs, and overall feasibility to ensure alignment with business and technical capabilities.

  • Comprehend Legacy System Domain: Understanding existing systems that will interface or be replaced by the new system is crucial for seamless transition and integration.

  • Define Scope (what's in, what's out): Clearly outline the project boundaries, features that will be included, and those that won't be part of the project.

  • Communication Plan (stakeholders): Establish a plan for how and when stakeholders will be informed and consulted throughout the project.

  • Understand & Enumerate Availability & Performance Expectations: Early identification of system performance and availability requirements will guide subsequent design and development activities.

Activities

  • Perform Domain Analysis using Various Methods and Sources: Utilize tools like SWOT analysis, market research, and stakeholder interviews to understand the domain thoroughly.

  • Determine Current System Gaps, Successes, and Expectations: Perform a detailed analysis to understand what is lacking in the current system, what is working well, and what stakeholders expect the new system to accomplish.

  • Discover Dependent Systems, Dependencies on Adjacent Systems: Map out how the new system will interact with existing systems and identify any dependencies that need to be addressed.

  • Uncover Expected Deltas in the Near & Medium Term (Domain Changes, User Population Changes, Expected Functional & Non-functional Changes): Anticipate future changes in user needs, domain shifts, and technology trends to build a more future-proof solution.

Questions

  • How are Compliance and Mandates Being Addressed?: Ensure that all legal and organizational policies and mandates are being considered and planned for.

  • What Insights Have Been Gained About the Domain?: Recap what has been learned about the business or operational domain where the system will exist.

  • How are Alignment Gaps Identified and Addressed?: Establish methods for spotting and resolving discrepancies between stakeholder expectations and project deliverables.

  • What are the Operational Environment Details, Including Dependencies?: Document the system's operational requirements, including hardware, software, and network dependencies, to ensure that all necessary resources are available for successful deployment.

This phase sets the foundation for the entire project, and its thorough execution is critical for the success of all subsequent phases.

Planning & Risk Mitigation

Objectives

  • Remediate Risk to Acceptable Levels: The planning phase involves identifying all possible risks and creating strategies to either eliminate or minimize them to an acceptable level.

  • Demonstrate Architectural/Design Efficacy: Establish the viability of your architectural and design choices by means of prototypes, models, or empirical data.

  • Transition Plan (Progress, Quality, Metrics, etc.): Develop a clear plan outlining how you will transition from one phase to the next, specifying metrics for progress, quality, and other critical factors.

Activities

  • Dev & QA Team Members, Roles, Responsibilities:

    • Ramp up, familiarization & planning: Establish the development and quality assurance teams, outlining specific roles and responsibilities for each team member.

  • Transition Plan:

    • Devise transition execution strategy: Measure progress, correctness/completeness, and handle exceptional events.

  • Compliance & Mandate (Alignment With): Ensure the project aligns with all legal and organizational requirements.

  • Architecture/Design Acceptance:

    • Align design with Domain E/R analysis and non-functional expectations to secure stakeholder acceptance.

  • Risk List Refinement, Risk Remediation: Revisit the risk list to ensure all risks are being appropriately managed.

  • Scope Refinement: Revisit and, if necessary, adjust the project scope.

  • Populate a Product Backlog: List all the components/features that need to be implemented.

  • Construction Planning:

    • Quality Assurance:
      • Publish/share coding guidelines, including coding standards, naming conventions, and best practices.
      • Set unit test code coverage thresholds and expectations.
      • Plan code review strategies.
    • Group product backlog items into construction phases.
    • Define construction phase deliverables & milestones.

  • Architectural Prototype: Design a working prototype to mitigate design risks.

  • Security Risks: Make sure all security risks and threats are adequately addressed.

Questions

  • How are Risks Identified and Mitigated?: Ensure you have a concrete plan for identifying and handling risks.

  • What are the Construction and Transition Plans?: Make clear what the road map looks like for building and transitioning the product.

  • How are Roles and Responsibilities Defined for the Dev and QA Teams?: Confirm that every team member knows their role and what is expected of them.

  • How is Compliance Ensured, and Architecture/Design Accepted?: Validate that the project will comply with all legal and organizational mandates, and that its architecture and design are sound and accepted by stakeholders.

The Planning & Risk Mitigation phase serves as the blueprint for the project, guiding all subsequent activities and serving as a reference for decision-making. It plays a pivotal role in ensuring that the project stays on track and achieves its objectives while minimizing risks and obstacles.

Construction & Transition

Construction

Objectives

  • Build, Test, Integrate: The primary goals during the construction phase are to write the code, test it at various levels, and integrate it into the existing system or architecture.

  • Adjust Risk, Cost, Progress, Delivery Schedule: Continuously monitor and adjust risk levels, project costs, progress, and the delivery schedule based on real-world metrics and findings.

Activities

  • Code Implementation: Developers write the code based on approved designs and architecture.

  • Testing (Unit, Integration): Rigorous unit and integration tests are conducted to ensure code quality and functionality.

  • Review and Integrate Changes:

    • Monitor construction progress and velocity.
    • Monitor Transition Plan Execution, Verification & Validation metrics.
    • Adjust estimated cost and delivery schedule based on real-world data.

  • Inform Stakeholders: Keep all stakeholders updated about progress, challenges, and the overall health of the project.

Questions

  • What is the Development and Testing Process?: Are there established methodologies and procedures for development and testing?

  • How are Milestones Tracked and Met?: What mechanisms are in place to track progress against milestones?

  • What Quality Assurance Measures are in Place?: What steps are being taken to ensure the highest quality output?

Transition

Objectives

  • Migrate Data: Safely transfer all necessary data from the old system to the new one, if applicable.

  • Transition Users: Train and move users from the old system to the new one.

  • Monitor Transition Progress: Keep a close eye on how the transition is progressing, and make adjustments as necessary.

Activities

  • Data Migration Strategy: What approach will be used to move data from the old system to the new one?

  • User Transition and Training: How will users be moved to the new system, and what training will be provided?

  • Transition Integrity Validation: Ensure that the transition has been carried out accurately, and all systems function as expected.

Questions

  • What Data Validation Techniques Will Be Used During Migration?: How will you ensure the integrity of data during its migration?

  • How Will User Training and Adoption Be Managed?: What strategies are in place for making sure users can easily transition to the new system?

  • What Metrics Are Being Used to Monitor Transition Success?: How will you know if the transition has been successful?

The Construction & Transition phase is critical for bringing the planned software to life and ensuring its successful integration into the existing ecosystem. It's a multifaceted stage where coding, testing, and transitioning all need to be managed effectively to ensure the project's overall success.

Deployment & Stabilization

Deployment

Objectives

  • Deploy to Target Environment: The foremost goal is to successfully deploy the newly built or updated software to the target environment.

  • Validate Deployment Success: Verify that the deployment has succeeded and that the new system or feature is functioning as expected.

  • Ensure SLA Compliance: Ensure that the deployment meets any Service Level Agreements (SLAs) that may be in place.

Activities

  • Deploy Artifacts: Physically move all necessary code, data, and other artifacts to the target environment.

  • Monitor Deployment Success: Continuously watch for any issues that may arise during or immediately after the deployment process.

  • Validate Performance Metrics: Assess whether the new deployment meets the predetermined performance metrics and KPIs.

Questions

  • What is the Deployment Process?: What are the steps to move from a staging environment to production?

  • How is the Success/Failure of the Deployment Validated?: What metrics or indicators are being used to confirm a successful deployment?

  • How are SLA Compliance and Performance Metrics Ensured?: What checks are in place to make sure the deployment is compliant with SLAs and performance expectations?

Stabilization

Objectives

  • Ensure System Stability: Make certain that the deployed system is stable and not causing disruptions.

  • Optimize Performance: Make any necessary tweaks to improve the system's performance.

  • Resolve any Outstanding Issues: Address any remaining problems or imperfections in the deployed system.

Activities

  • Monitor System Stability: Keep an eye on various system metrics to ensure ongoing stability.

  • Optimize as Needed: Fine-tune the system to improve performance, based on real-world data.

  • Address Any Post-Deployment Issues: Handle any issues that become evident after the deployment is complete.

Questions

  • How is System Stability Ensured?: What metrics or indicators are used to monitor system stability?

  • What Measures are Taken to Optimize Performance?: What tools or techniques are employed to enhance system performance?

  • How are Post-Deployment Issues Resolved?: What is the process for identifying and addressing issues that arise after deployment?

The Deployment & Stabilization phase is critical for transitioning from a software product that works "on paper" or in a controlled environment to one that successfully serves its intended audience in the real world. It involves not only the technical activities of deploying software but also the often-overlooked yet equally vital activities of monitoring and optimizing it to ensure it meets both functional and business goals.

Retrospection

Objectives

  • Evaluate Project Success: Assess whether the project met its stated goals, including timelines, budget, and scope.

  • Identify Lessons Learned: Document what worked well and what didn't, to improve future projects.

  • Measure ROI: Evaluate the return on investment and whether the project delivered on its value proposition.

Activities

  • Project Review: Conduct a comprehensive review with stakeholders to evaluate the success of the project based on predefined criteria.

  • Gather Feedback: Collect feedback from team members, stakeholders, and users to understand the strengths and weaknesses of the project.

  • Documentation: Create a formal 'lessons learned' document or database entry for institutional knowledge.

  • ROI Analysis: Compare the benefits gained against the costs incurred in the project to calculate the Return on Investment.

  • Stakeholder Communication: Share the outcomes, lessons learned, and ROI with all stakeholders to close the loop.

Questions

  • Was the Project Successful by Predefined Metrics?: Did the project meet its goals in terms of timeline, budget, and quality?

  • What Lessons Have Been Learned for Future Projects?: What insights can be carried forward to future initiatives?

  • How Has the Project Affected Business Metrics or KPIs?: Has there been a measurable impact on the business, either positive or negative?

  • What Would You Do Differently Next Time?: Based on the retrospective, what changes would you implement in future projects?

  • Was the ROI in Line with Expectations?: Was the return on investment as predicted, better, or worse?

The Retrospection phase serves as a closing chapter that allows the team and stakeholders to pause and evaluate how the project went. It provides an opportunity for introspection, learning, and even course-correction for future projects. The activities in this phase ensure that the project's impact is measured, that lessons are learned and documented, and that the project's success or failure is understood in a nuanced way that benefits future endeavors.
